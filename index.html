Data Collection

As with any data science project we will need a dataset to analyze.

The one we are using is from Data.gov, namely Biodiversity by County - Distribution of Animals, Plants and Natural Communities (https://catalog.data.gov/dataset/biodiversity-by-county-distribution-of-animals-plants-and-natural-communities). Its a collection of various species of fauna and flora found in the state of New York and their respective conservation statuses, compiled by the NYS Department of Environmental Conservation (DEC) over the course of decades (hence why there is an entire column dedicated to the year a population was last documented).

Ecosystems are a popular point of observation, especially when the health of our environment relies on them. A crucial aspect of that is biodiversity: the variety of organisms that live in a specified area. This encompasses plants, microorganisms, animals, etc.

For this tutorial however, we will merely be focusing on animals.

You will notice two kinds of conservation statuses assigned to each species: state and global. The key thing to keep in mind is that the first letter dictates to which it is referring to, and the number indicates the status of the population itself (with 1 - Critically Imperiled, to 5 - Secure). Statuses that end in X are Presumed Extirpated or Extinct. Keep in mind that in terms of state boundaries extirpation does not equate to extinction.

What about state rankings relative to taxonomic groups? There could be some interesting patterns to discover there.

For simplicity's sake we will only be relying on concrete rankings. That is: S1, S2, S3, S4, S5. We will be ignoring ranges, and those that may be inexact (ending with '?'). Let's start off with a table:

What becomes quite apparent however, is that total observations between groups vary greatly enough for it to be a distraction. It'd be more difficult to form comparisons from this.

What we could do to circumvent this issue is divide each value by the total number of documented species within that taxonomic group. That way, we compare proportions relative to each.

In[]:
# New dataframe for altered data.
new_srank = pd.DataFrame()
count = []
new_cols = [[], [], [], [], []]
for i, row in srank_table.iterrows():
    count.append(row["S1"] + row["S2"] + row["S3"] + row["S4"] + row["S5"])
    if (count[i] != 0):
        new_cols[0].append(float(row["S1"]) / count[i])
        new_cols[1].append(float(row["S2"]) / count[i])
        new_cols[2].append(float(row["S3"]) / count[i])
        new_cols[3].append(float(row["S4"]) / count[i])
        new_cols[4].append(float(row["S5"]) / count[i])
    else:
        new_cols[0].append(0.0)
        new_cols[1].append(0.0)
        new_cols[2].append(0.0)
        new_cols[3].append(0.0)
        new_cols[4].append(0.0)

for i in range(5):
    new_srank[srank_cols[i]] = new_cols[i]
new_srank["Taxonomic Group"] = srank_table["Taxonomic Group"]
new_srank["total"] = count

Analysis, Hypothesis Testing, Machine Learning

The primary purpose behind hypothesis testing is to determine whether we can reject or approve the null hypothesis. As our focus has been on state conservation rankings relative to taxonomic groups, that would be: taxonomic grouping does not indicate or affect the likelihood of a species's conservation status.

We achieve this by using machine learning models, which can help us find patterns in a dataset. Various kinds exist, such as SVM, regression, random forests... The point is that you will want to use the one that best fits your dataset.
